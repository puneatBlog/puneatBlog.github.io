Why is density of air lower at higher altitudes?

While we all know the general answer to this question, what could be the exact scientific explantion for this? Dive into 11th grade chemistry with me as I try to answer this question in depth!

Posted by Puneet on December 01, 2020

We have all learned that the density of air is lower at higher altitudes. But why exactly is that? We’ll begin with the basics. Density is an important property of any gas, which includes our air. It is defined as the mass of an object divided by its volume.

$$D={M \over V}$$ Where,
$$D = Density$$ $$M = Mass$$ $$V = Volume$$

However, most of our experiences with density involve solids. We have already seen objects that are heavier than other objects, even though they are the same size.

Do you remember that trick question we used to ask our friends- ‘What is heavier, 1kg of steel or 1 kg of a feather?’. Of course, they’re both equally heavy, with both having a weight of 1kg. But as steel is denser, we can fit 1kg of steel in the palm of our hands but it’ll probably take an entire room to fill 1kg worth of feathers.

For solids, because the molecules are bound to one another, the density of an object remains almost the same anywhere. A brick at the sea-level would have the same density as the same brick on top of the Mt. Everest. But when we deal with gases, density can vary widely, as the molecules are free to move about. Air at the surface of the earth has a very different density than air 50 kilometres above the earth.

Air Pressure

Before dealing with air density, let's talk about air pressure.

We know that air pressure also decreases with increase in altitude. Air pressure can be considered as the weight of the air over a given location. In figure 1, we consider air pressure to be the weight of air over the cuboidal unit area. As we go higher up the atmosphere, there is some air below us and some air above us. But there is always less air above us than was present at a lower altitude.

Figure 1.

For example, there are fewer molecules above the 50 km surface than are found above the 12 km surface, which is why the pressure is less at 50 km.

Therefore, air pressure decreases as we increase altitude.

Air Temperature

Now let’s try to understand the relationship between air temperature and altitude.

The atmosphere is divided into different layers depending on how temperature changes.

Figure 2. Temperature Changes In Atmospheric Regions.

If we follow the temperature line in figure 2 above, moving to the left represents a decrease in temperature, and to the right represents an increase in temperature.

  • Troposphere: The Weather Layer

    • Almost all the changes in our weather occur in this region which extends approximately 12 km straight up. The troposphere is also the closest layer to Earth. The heat from the sun rays is absorbed by the ground and warms the air at the surface. Hence the air is warmest at sea level and gradually becomes colder as we move upward.

    Thus, as we climb a mountain, we notice the air temperature to decrease by 6.5 degrees C for every 1000 meters we go up.

    Figure 3. Temperature Change With Altitude.

    Therefore, air temperature also decreases as we increase altitude.
