Why is the density of air lower at higher altitudes?

While we all know the general answer to this question, what could be the exact scientific explanation for this? Dive into 11th grade chemistry with me as I try to answer this question in depth!

Posted by Puneet on December 01, 2020

We have all learned that the density of air is lower at higher altitudes. But why exactly is that? We’ll begin with the basics. Density is an important property of any gas, which includes our air. It is defined as the mass of an object divided by its volume.

$$D={M \over V} \hspace{1cm} \textrm{(1)}$$ Where,
$$D = Density$$ $$M = Mass$$ $$V = Volume$$

However, most of our experiences with density involve solids. We have already seen objects that are heavier than other objects, even though they are the same size.

Do you remember that trick question we used to ask our friends- ‘What is heavier, 1kg of steel or 1 kg of a feather?’. Of course, they’re both equally heavy, with both having a weight of 1kg. But as steel is denser, we can fit 1kg of steel in the palm of our hands but it’ll probably take an entire room to fill 1kg worth of feathers.

For solids, because the molecules are bound to one another, the density of an object remains almost the same anywhere. A brick at the sea-level would have the same density as the same brick on top of the Mt. Everest. But when we deal with gases, density can vary widely, as the molecules are free to move about. Air at the surface of the earth has a very different density than air 50 kilometres above the earth.

Air Pressure

Before dealing with air density, let's talk about air pressure.

We know that air pressure also decreases with increase in altitude. Air pressure can be considered as the weight of the air over a given location. In figure 1, we consider air pressure to be the weight of air over the cuboidal unit area. As we go higher up the atmosphere, there is some air below us and some air above us. But there is always less air above us than was present at a lower altitude.

Figure 1.

For example, there are fewer molecules above the 50 km surface than are found above the 12 km surface, which is why the pressure is less at 50 km.

Therefore, air pressure decreases as we increase altitude.

Air Temperature

Now let’s try to understand the relationship between air temperature and altitude.

The atmosphere is divided into different layers depending on how temperature changes.

Figure 2. Temperature Changes In Atmospheric Regions.

If we follow the temperature line in figure 2 above, moving to the left represents a decrease in temperature, and to the right represents an increase in temperature.

  • Troposphere: The Weather Layer

    • Almost all the changes in our weather occur in this region which extends approximately 12 km straight up. The troposphere is also the closest layer to Earth. The heat from the sun rays is absorbed by the ground and warms the air at the surface. Hence the air is warmest at sea level and gradually becomes colder as we move upward.

    Thus, as we climb a mountain, we notice the air temperature to decrease by 6.5 degrees C for every 1000 meters we go up.

    Figure 3. Temperature Change With Altitude.

    Therefore, air temperature also decreases as we increase altitude.

    Finally: Air Density

    While there are a lot of intuitive and easy-to-understand reasons for why air density decreases with increase in temperature, we won’t address them. Why? Because they’re too generic and don’t really answer the WHY. They instead simply state a generic fact such as ‘due to gravity’ an offer no concrete explanations.

    Instead, we’ll try to link what we understood about air pressure, altitude, and air temperature with air density.

    Before we do that, we should probably go through a bit of math and chemistry. In particular, the Ideal Gas Equation / Gas State Law. For convenience, we will refer to gas as air.

    Kinetic theory of gases states that the air is composed of a large number of molecules that are very small relative to the distance between those molecules. These air molecules are in constant, random motion and frequently collide with each other and with the walls of a container. Note that this container is imaginary in context to the atmosphere and it's volume keeps on changing with altitude. Try to think why (Hint: look at the shape of Earth).

    Figure 4. Kinetic Theory of Gases.
    The balls resemble air molecules and color represents different components of air.

    Now the Ideal Gas Law states that,

    $$PV = nRT \hspace{1cm} \textrm{(2)}$$ where,
    $$P = \textrm{Air Pressure}$$ $$V = \textrm{Volume Occupied by Air Molecules}$$ $$n = \textrm{Number of Moles (Quantity of Air)}$$ $$R = \textrm{Universal Gas Constant}$$ $$T = \textrm{Absolute Temperature of Air (273.15 + Temp(in Celsius) )}$$

    We also know that since air is a gas, the ‘quantity of air’ is the same everywhere. This means the mass of air remains almost constant throughout everywhere.

    Now let’s consider the volume occupied by air molecules at sea level. Therefore,

    $$P_{sealevel} \times V_{sealevel} = n \times R \times T_{sealevel}$$ $$V_{sealevel} = {{n \times R \times T_{sealevel}} \over {P_{sealevel}}} \hspace{1cm} \textrm{(3)}$$

    Similarly the volume occupied by air molecules at 10,000 feet is,

    $$P_{10,000ft} \times V_{10,000ft} = n \times R \times T_{10,000ft}$$ $$V_{10,000ft} = {{n \times R \times T_{10,000ft}} \over {P_{10,000ft}}} \hspace{1cm} \textrm{(4)}$$

    To find the difference in volume between both the altitudes, we subtract equation (2) from (3)

    $$V_{sealevel} - V_{10,000ft} = {{n \times R \times {{(T_{sealevel}-T_{10,000ft})} \over {(P_{sealevel} - P_{10,000ft})}} \hspace{1cm} \textrm{(5)}}}$$

    Now because R is constant and n is also constant, we ignore these and replace the '=' sign with the proportionality sign,

    $$(V_{sealevel} - V_{10,000ft}) \propto {{{{(T_{sealevel}-T_{10,000ft})} \over {(P_{sealevel} - P_{10,000ft})}} \hspace{1cm} \textrm{(6)}}}$$

    Values at sealevel minus the values at 10,000ft is nothing but the change in values, hence, we'll replace them with the delta operator (signifying the change),

    $$ \Delta V \propto {\Delta T \over \Delta P} \hspace{1cm} \textrm{(7)}$$

    Now as we can observe, change in volume occupied by air molecules is directly proportional to change in air temperature and inversely proportional to change in air pressure.

    This would mean that volume occupied by air molecules increases with an increase in air temperature and decreases with decrease in air temperature.

    $$ \uparrow \Delta V \propto {\uparrow \Delta T} $$ $$ \downarrow \Delta V \propto {\downarrow \Delta T}$$

    Similarly, volume occupied by air molecules decreases with an increase in air pressure and increases with decrease in air pressure.

    $$ \uparrow \Delta V \propto {\downarrow \Delta P}$$ $$ \downarrow \Delta V \propto {\uparrow \Delta P}$$

    Remember we observed before that air pressure decreases at a much faster rate than air temperature, with an increase in altitude? Now is the time to use that inference.

    Since we now know that both air pressure and air temperature decrease with increase in altitude and that air pressure decreases at a much faster rate than the air temperature, we observe that the denominator term on the RHS in equation (7) decreases faster than the numerator term. This leads to the value of the entire expression on RHS to slowly increase, thus leading to an increase in volume occupied.

    We can verify this by taking the values of air pressure and temperature at two different altitudes from the table above. A value of greater than 1 for V in equation 7 would mean that volume increased as we went up and a value of less than 1 would mean that volume decreased as we went up.

    $$(V_{sealevel} - V_{10,000ft}) \propto {{{{(288-268.17)} \over {(29.92 - 20.57)}}}}$$ $$(V_{sealevel} - V_{10,000ft}) \propto {{{{19.83} \over {9.35}}}}$$ $$(V_{sealevel} - V_{10,000ft}) \propto {2.12} \hspace{1cm} \textrm{(8)}$$

    As we can see from equation (8), volume occupied by air molecules increases with an increase in altitude. With this clear, we now use the formula for density to finally find the relationship between air density and altitude.

    Recall from equation (1), the formula for density.

    $$D={M \over V}$$

    As we can see, with the mass of air constant, density of air is inversely proportional to its volume occupied. In other words, with an increase in volume occupied by the air molecules, its density decreases.

    $$ \downarrow \Delta D \propto {\uparrow \Delta V}$$ $$ \textrm{When Altitude Increases} \uparrow, $$ $$\textrm{Volume Increases}\uparrow V$$$$ \textrm{Thus Density Decreases} {\downarrow D}$$

    Therefore, as volume occupied by air molecules increases with increase in altitude, air density also decreases.

    We have finally connected the concepts of pressure, gas-law, temperature, density, and geography to mathematically prove that air density does decrease with increase in altitude.
